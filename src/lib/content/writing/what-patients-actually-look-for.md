I’ve sat with a lot of dentists who think their website needs to communicate
philosophy. Patient-centered care. Cutting-edge technology. A commitment
to excellence. There’s usually a paragraph about it on the home page, in
italics, signed.

Nobody reads that paragraph.

Here’s what patients actually want to know, in order:

1. Are you open right now, or when can I be seen?
2. Where are you, exactly?
3. Do you take my insurance?
4. Can I book an appointment without calling?
5. Do you look like a person I’d trust to do something to my mouth?

That’s the list. You can fit all five answers above the fold. The
mission statement can go on the About page, where it belongs, and where
people can ignore it.

The one that surprises dentists most is the last one. They expect
patients to care about their education or their continuing-ed credits.
What patients are actually doing is looking at a photo of you, looking at
the inside of your office, and asking themselves a gut question: would
this person be careful with me?

The answer to that question doesn’t come from copy. It comes from
photos, the kind of photos that look like they were taken by a human
being who knew the place. Not headshots in front of a beige wall. Not
stock images of someone else’s smile. Photos of your actual office, your
actual team, on a normal Tuesday.
